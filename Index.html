Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saved millions

Here's a time of Dr. Borloug's life:

  • 1914 - Born in Cresco, lowa
  • 1933 - Leaves his family's farm to attend the University Of Minnesota, thanks to a Depression era program known as the ''National Youth Administration''
  • 1935 - Has to stop school and save more money. Works in the Civilian Conservation Corps, heloing satrving Americans. '' I saw how food changed them'', he said '' All of this left scars on me.''
  • 1937 - Finishes University and takes a job in the US Forestry Service
  • 1938 - Marries wife of 69 years Magret Gibson. Get laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, he returns to school study stakman, who teaches him about breeding resistent plants.
  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the millitary after the school pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected. Instead, the millitary asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control maleria disinfectant, and other applied science
  • 1941 Recieves a Ph.D in Genetics and plant pathology
  • 1944 - Rejects a 100% salary increase for Dupoint, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth
  • 1945 - Discovers a way grown wheat twice each season doubling wheat yields
  • 1953 - Crosses a short study dwarf breed of wheat witha high-yielding American breed, creating a strain that respond well to fertilizer. It goes on to provide 95% Mexico wheat.
  • 1962 - Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding strains of wheat to the indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate maass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population
  • 1970 - recieves the Nobel Peace Price
  • 1983 - helps seven Africa countries dramatically increase their maize and sorghum yields
  • 1984 - becomes a distingushed professor at Texas A&M University
  • 2005 - states '' we will have to double the world food supply by 2050.'' Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we run out of the arable land. Says that GM crops are inherently dangerous because ''we've genetically modifying plantsand animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people selecting the best breeds''
  • 2009 - Dies at the age of 95
